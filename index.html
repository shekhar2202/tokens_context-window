How AI reads text
Tokens & Context Window
Two foundational ideas behind every AI language model. Understanding these helps you use AI tools far more effectively.
What is a token?
A token is the smallest unit of text an AI processes. Think of it like how a reader doesn't see letters — they see word-chunks. AI splits your input into these chunks before doing anything with it.

A token is usually a word, but common words like "the" may be a single token, while longer or rarer words like "unbelievable" might split into 2–3 tokens: un, believ, able.
What is a context window?
The context window is the AI's active memory — the total tokens it can hold and reason over at once. Everything outside this window is invisible to the model.

If a conversation grows longer than the window, the AI cannot "see" what was said earlier. It doesn't forget — those tokens were simply never shown to it again.
How text becomes tokens — a real example
Every sentence you type is split before the AI reads it. Here's what that looks like:
Sentence: "I love building AI apps" I love building AI apps = 5 tokens
Single word "unbelievable" — split into sub-word tokens un believ able = 3 tokens
Tokens also include spaces and punctuation. On average, 1 token ≈ 0.75 English words, so 1,000 words ≈ 1,300 tokens. This is how AI providers measure and price usage.
Token analogy Imagine reading a book, but you can only read in syllable-sized bites. "Saturday" becomes "Sat-ur-day". That's how AI reads — in token-sized bites, not whole sentences at once.
Context window analogy Imagine a sticky note with room for only 10 words. As you write more, older words fall off the top. The AI only knows what's on the note right now — nothing more.

Why this matters in real AI: GPT-4 has a ~128,000 token context window. Claude has up to 200,000 tokens. When you have a very long conversation or upload a large document, the AI may lose track of things said early on — not because it's forgetful, but because those tokens literally left the window. This is why summarizing long chats or breaking large tasks into smaller parts helps AI perform better.
